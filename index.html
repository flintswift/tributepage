Robert Smalls

- Robert Smalls was born into slavery in Beaufort, South Carolina, on April 5, 1839.

- As a slave, he worked as a house servant and later as a laborer on the docks and aboard ships.

- In May 1862, during the Civil War, Smalls commandeered a Confederate transport ship, the Planter, and sailed it past Confederate defenses to the Union blockade, freeing himself and several other enslaved people on board.

- Smalls became a national hero for his bravery and strategic thinking during the daring escape, which provided valuable intelligence to the Union Navy.

- After the war, Smalls settled in Beaufort and became involved in politics, advocating for voting rights for African Americans.

- In 1868, he was elected to the South Carolina House of Representatives and later served in the state senate and the U.S. House of Representatives.

- As a congressman, Smalls fought for civil rights and worked to improve conditions for African Americans in the South.

- He co-authored the 1875 Civil Rights Act, which prohibited discrimination in public accommodations, and helped pass the 1895 Naval Appropriations Act, which provided funding for historically Black colleges and universities.

- Smalls also championed economic development in the South, promoting the development of railroads and other infrastructure.

- He served as a delegate to several Republican National Conventions and was a close associate of President William McKinley.

- Smalls retired from politics in 1889 and went into business, investing in a range of ventures, including railroads, real estate, and hotels.

- He also served as the customs collector for the Port of Beaufort and as the director of the Freedmen's Bank.

- Smalls died in Beaufort on February 23, 1915, at the age of 75.

- His legacy as a pioneering African American politician and civil rights leader has been recognized through numerous honors, including the naming of a U.S. Navy ship in his honor.

- Smalls' story continues to inspire generations of people to fight for freedom, justice, and equality.
